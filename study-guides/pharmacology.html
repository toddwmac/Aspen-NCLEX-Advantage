Pharmacology Essentials for the NCLEX

Introduction to Pharmacology

Pharmacology is the study of drugs, their effects on the body, and their role in patient care. For NCLEX preparation, it is crucial to master:

  • Drug classifications
  • Mechanisms of action
  • Side effects
  • Nursing considerations
  • Patient education

Key Drug Classifications

Below is a breakdown of major drug classes you’ll encounter:

1. Analgesics (Pain Relievers)

  • Opioids: Morphine, Hydrocodone, Fentanyl
    Mechanism: Bind to opioid receptors, altering pain perception.
    Common Side Effects: Respiratory depression, constipation, sedation.
    Nursing Tip: Monitor respiratory rate closely; administer naloxone if needed.
  • Non-Opioids: Acetaminophen, NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen)
    Mechanism: Inhibit prostaglandin synthesis to reduce pain.
    Common Side Effects: Liver damage (acetaminophen) and gastrointestinal irritation (NSAIDs).
    Nursing Tip: Do not exceed recommended dosages.

2. Antibiotics (Fight Infections)

  • Penicillins: Amoxicillin, Penicillin G
    Mechanism: Interrupt bacterial cell wall synthesis.
    Common Side Effects: Allergic reactions such as rashes or anaphylaxis.
    Nursing Tip: Always assess for potential allergies.
  • Aminoglycosides: Gentamicin, Tobramycin
    Mechanism: Disrupt bacterial protein synthesis.
    Common Side Effects: Ototoxicity and nephrotoxicity.
    Nursing Tip: Monitor kidney function and hearing.

3. Antihypertensives (Lower Blood Pressure)

  • ACE Inhibitors: Lisinopril, Enalapril
    Mechanism: Block the conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II.
    Common Side Effects: Dry cough, hyperkalemia, and angioedema.
    Nursing Tip: Monitor for developing cough and electrolyte imbalances.
  • Beta-Blockers: Metoprolol, Atenolol
    Mechanism: Reduce heart rate by blocking beta-adrenergic receptors.
    Common Side Effects: Bradycardia, fatigue, and hypotension.
    Nursing Tip: Check the patient’s heart rate prior to administration.
